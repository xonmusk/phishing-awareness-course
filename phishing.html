Scenario 1
The Urgent Account Verification
A routine Tuesday morning. An urgent email from IT Security. 2 hours to act, or your account gets suspended. Something feels off, but the clock is ticking.
Attack Vector: Email Phishing | Difficulty: Standard
Scenario 1 of 3
The Urgent Account Verification

It is 9:47 AM on a Tuesday. You just settled into your desk. This email landed in your inbox 3 minutes ago. Read it carefully.

Outlook
URGENT: Account Verification Required, Immediate Action Needed
IT
IT Security Team
<it-security@jprnorgan-alerts.com>

Dear Employee,

Our security monitoring systems have detected unauthorized access attempts on your corporate account. As part of our mandatory security protocol, you must verify your credentials within the next 2 hours or your account will be temporarily suspended.

Please click the link below to verify your identity through our secure portal:

https://jpmorgan-secure-verify.accounts-center.com/auth

This is a time-sensitive security matter. Failure to complete verification will result in restricted access to all corporate systems, including email, trading platforms, and client databases.

Do not share this email or forward it to colleagues, as the verification link is unique to your account.

Michael Torres
Information Security Division
Corporate Technology Services
Automated security notification. Ref: SEC-2026-4471
What would you do?
Select one option below
A
Click the link immediately. You do not want to lose access to your systems, and the email says you only have 2 hours.
B
Forward this email to a colleague to ask if they received the same message. A second opinion could help you decide.
C
Do not click anything. Report this email to the Cybersecurity team using the Report Phishing button and notify your manager.

Consequence Video

Credentials Compromised

You clicked the link and entered your credentials on what looked like a legitimate portal. Within minutes, an attacker used your credentials to access internal systems. The sender domain was "jprnorgan" (misspelled). The URL "accounts-center.com" is not a JPMorgan domain. The 2 hour deadline and "do not share" instruction are classic social engineering pressure tactics.

You Spread the Threat

By forwarding the phishing email, you exposed a colleague to the same attack. If they click the link, two accounts are compromised instead of one. The "do not share" instruction was designed to prevent you from checking with IT. Checking with a peer instead of security was the wrong escalation path.

Excellent Instinct

You resisted the urgency, did not click, and followed the correct protocol: report to Cybersecurity, notify your manager. The email contained 5 red flags: spoofed domain ("jprnorgan"), fake URL, urgency and threat language, artificial deadline, and an isolation tactic ("do not share").

Scenario 1: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags in This Email
Spoofed sender domain. "jprnorgan-alerts.com" is not the real jpmorgan.com. Always check the actual email address, not the display name.
Urgency and alarm language. "URGENT," "Immediate Action Needed" are designed to bypass your rational thinking.
Artificial time pressure. "Within the next 2 hours" creates panic. Real security protocols do not impose arbitrary deadlines via email.
Suspicious URL. "accounts-center.com" is not a JPMorgan domain. The URL uses "jpmorgan" as a prefix to appear legitimate.
Isolation tactic. "Do not share this email" is designed to prevent you from consulting IT or colleagues.
What You Should Do Instead
Do not click any links. Do not reply. Do not forward the email.
Use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook to send the email with full headers to the Cybersecurity team.
Notify your direct manager verbally or through a separate communication channel.
If you already clicked, call the Cybersecurity Hotline immediately. Speed matters.

C

Scenario 2: Consequence

Consequence Video

[Replace with consequence video for chosen path]

$47,500 Sent to a Fraudulent Account

This was a Business Email Compromise (BEC) attack. The email came from "jpmorqan" (note the "q" instead of "g"). There is no Meridian Consulting Group. The money is gone and likely unrecoverable. BEC attacks caused $2.77 billion in U.S. losses in 2024. This is exactly how they work: authority, urgency, secrecy, and bypassed approval chains.

Attack Prevented

You called Robert directly using a known number from the company directory. The real Robert Chen has no idea about any wire transfer. By verifying through an independent channel, you just prevented a $47,500 loss. This is the "validate, validate, validate" principle in action.

Risky Escalation

Forwarding to your manager is better than acting alone, but you have now introduced the phishing email to another person's inbox. Your manager might process it, thinking the SVP already cleared it. The correct action is to verify with the supposed sender directly using a known phone number. Never forward suspicious requests. Verify them independently.

Scenario 2: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags in This Request
Spoofed domain. "jpmorqan-internal.com" swaps the "g" for a "q". This is nearly invisible at a glance.
Bypassed approval chain. Asking you to skip normal wire authorization procedures is always a red flag, no matter who is asking.
Secrecy request. "Do not discuss this with anyone else on the team" is a classic isolation tactic to prevent verification.
Unfamiliar vendor. A new vendor you have never heard of with brand new wire details is a major warning sign.
End of day pressure. "Before end of business today" prevents you from taking time to verify properly.
What You Should Do Instead
Never process a wire transfer based solely on an email, especially if it bypasses normal approval workflows.
Call the requester directly using a number from the company directory. Not the number in the email. Not a reply to the email.
Any request involving secrecy around financial transactions should be treated as suspicious and reported immediately.
The more senior the supposed sender, the more important it is to verify independently.

The Trusted Colleague

It is Monday morning. You get a message on Microsoft Teams from Priya Sharma, a colleague you have worked with for two years. Read the conversation.

Click the link and log in. It is Priya, you have worked together for years, and she says she already did it.
B
Do not click the link. Walk over to Priya's desk or call her directly to ask about it in person.

Reply on Teams asking Priya to confirm the URL is legitimate before you click.
Scenario 3: Consequence
▶

Consequence Video

[Replace with consequence video for chosen path]

Credential Harvesting via Compromised Account

Priya's account was hacked. She did not send those messages. An attacker used her legitimate Teams account to trick you. The link leads to a fake HR portal that captures your corporate credentials. 58% of phishing emails now come from compromised legitimate accounts. This is the hardest attack to detect because there are no obvious external red flags. The sender is real, the platform is real, and the tone matches the person.

This Saved You

You walked over to Priya and asked in person. She had no idea what you were talking about. Her account has been compromised. You immediately reported it to Cybersecurity, and they locked her account within 8 minutes, preventing the attacker from reaching anyone else. Out-of-band verification (using a different channel than the one the message came through) is the only reliable way to detect compromised account attacks.

Wrong Channel for Verification

You replied to Priya on Teams, but the attacker controls her Teams account. They will simply reply "Yeah it is legit, I just did mine!" and you will click. When an account is compromised, any reply through that same channel is coming from the attacker. The only safe verification is out-of-band: in person, by phone, or through a completely separate platform that the attacker does not control.

Scenario 3: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags in This Conversation
Suspicious URL. "secureworkspace.net" is not a JPMorgan domain. Even if a trusted colleague sends it, the link itself must be verified.
Request for corporate credentials. Any link asking you to enter your login should be independently verified, regardless of who sent it.
Same day deadline. "The deadline is today" creates pressure to act without checking, even from a friend.
Unusual request pattern. Did Priya normally send you HR portal links? If this is not a typical interaction pattern, treat it as suspicious.
What You Should Do Instead
Verify in person or by phone. Walk to their desk or call them. Do not use the same channel (Teams) that the suspicious message came through.
Navigate to any internal portal directly through the company intranet or your bookmarks. Never through a link someone sends you.
If you confirm the account is compromised, report it to Cybersecurity immediately so they can lock the account and protect others.
Remember: trust the person, but always verify the message through a separate channel.
Key Takeaway
The STOP Framework

Before acting on any request for credentials, transfers, or sensitive data, STOP and check:

S
Sender
Check the actual email address, not the display name. Look for misspellings, unusual domains, or slight character swaps.
T
Threat Language
Urgency, fear, consequences like "your account will be suspended" are hallmarks of social engineering. Real communications do not threaten you.
O
Odd Links
Hover before clicking. Does the domain match the real company URL? If there is any doubt, navigate directly through your browser.
P
Pressure to Act
Tight deadlines, "do not tell anyone," requests to bypass processes. All pressure tactics. Slow down and verify independently.

Always check the actual sender address, not the display name. Legitimate IT teams never impose arbitrary deadlines via email, never ask you to verify credentials through a link, and never tell you to keep it secret.

Trust the person, verify the message. Even messages from colleagues you have known for years can be attacks if their account is compromised. Always verify unusual requests through a different communication channel.

Reporting Procedure

1
DO NOT click any links, open attachments, or reply to the message.
2
Click the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook. This sends the full email with headers to the Cybersecurity team.
3
Notify your manager verbally or through a separate channel. Do not forward the suspicious email.
4
If you already clicked or shared credentials, call the Cybersecurity Hotline immediately. The faster you report, the faster the team can contain the breach.
