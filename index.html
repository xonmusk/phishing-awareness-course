Credentials Compromised

You clicked the link and entered your credentials on a fake portal. Within minutes, an attacker used them to access internal systems. The sender domain was "jprnorgan" (misspelled). The URL "accounts-center.com" is not a JPMorgan domain.

You Spread the Threat

By forwarding the phishing email, you exposed a colleague to the same attack. If they click, two accounts are compromised. The "do not share" instruction was designed to prevent you from checking with IT.

Excellent Instinct

You resisted the urgency, did not click, and followed the correct protocol: report to Cybersecurity, notify your manager. The email contained 5 red flags we will review next.

Scenario 1: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags
Spoofed domain. "jprnorgan-alerts.com" is not jpmorgan.com.
Urgency language. "URGENT" and "Immediate Action" bypass rational thinking.
Artificial deadline. "Within 2 hours" creates panic.
Suspicious URL. "accounts-center.com" is not JPMorgan.
Isolation tactic. "Do not share" prevents you from getting help.
What to Do Instead
Do not click any links. Do not reply. Do not forward.
Use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook.
Notify your manager verbally or through a separate channel.
If you already clicked, call the Cybersecurity Hotline immediately.

$47,500 Sent to a Fraudulent Account

This was a BEC attack. The email came from "jpmorqan" (the "q" instead of "g"). There is no Meridian Consulting Group. The money is gone. BEC attacks caused $2.77 billion in U.S. losses in 2024.

Attack Prevented

You called Robert directly using a known number. He has no idea about any wire transfer. By verifying through an independent channel, you prevented a $47,500 loss.

Risky Escalation

Forwarding to your manager is better than acting alone, but you introduced the phishing email to another inbox. The correct action is to verify with the sender directly using a known phone number.

Scenario 2: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags
Spoofed domain. "jpmorqan" swaps "g" for "q".
Bypassed approvals. Asking to skip normal wire authorization.
Secrecy request. "Do not discuss with anyone" prevents verification.
Unfamiliar vendor. New vendor with new wire details.
End of day pressure. "Before end of business" prevents proper checks.
What to Do Instead
Never process a wire transfer based solely on an email.
Call the requester using a number from the company directory.
Any request involving secrecy around money should be treated as suspicious.
The more senior the sender, the more important it is to verify.

Credentials Harvested

Priya's account was hacked. She did not send those messages. The link leads to a fake HR portal that captured your credentials. 58% of phishing now comes from compromised legitimate accounts.

Attack Stopped

You walked over to Priya. She had no idea. Her account was compromised. You reported it and Cybersecurity locked it within 8 minutes. Out-of-band verification is the only reliable defense.

Wrong Channel

You replied on Teams, but the attacker controls that account. They will simply reply "Yeah it is legit!" The only safe verification is through a completely separate channel: in person, by phone, or a different platform.

Scenario 3: Key Signals
Red Flags to Spot. Actions to Take.
Red Flags
Suspicious URL. "secureworkspace.net" is not JPMorgan.
Credential request. Any link asking for your login should be verified.
Same day deadline. "The deadline is today" creates urgency.
Unusual pattern. Does Priya normally send you HR links?
What to Do Instead
Verify in person or by phone. Never use the same channel.
Navigate to portals directly through the intranet or bookmarks.
If the account is compromised, report to Cybersecurity immediately.
Trust the person. Always verify the message separately.
Key Framework
Remember: STOP Before You Act

Before acting on any request for credentials, transfers, or sensitive data:

S
Sender
Check the actual email address. Look for misspellings or unusual domains.
T
Threat Language
Urgency, fear, "your account will be suspended" are social engineering hallmarks.
O
Odd Links
Hover before clicking. Does the domain match the real company?
P
Pressure to Act
Tight deadlines, secrecy, bypassed processes. Slow down and verify.

Scenario 1: Email Phishing
Always check the actual sender address. Legitimate IT teams never impose arbitrary deadlines via email, never ask you to verify credentials through a link, and never tell you to keep it secret.
Scenario 2: Business Email Compromise
Any request to bypass normal approval processes is a red flag. The more senior the sender appears, the more important it is to verify. Always call using a number you look up yourself.
Scenario 3: Compromised Internal Account
Trust the person, verify the message. Even colleagues you have known for years can be compromised. Always verify through a different communication channel.
